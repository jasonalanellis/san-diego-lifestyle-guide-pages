Best Beaches in San Diego: A Local's Guide to the Coast

San Diego has 70 miles of coastline, and not all of it is equal. Some beaches are built for swimming, others for watching sunsets, others for long trail walks where you barely see another person. This guide covers the ones worth knowing — and what makes each of them different.

Spending time at San Diego's beaches is one of those experiences that looks deceptively simple from the outside — show up, find sand, enjoy. But residents learn quickly that the beaches here have distinct personalities, distinct crowds, and very different conditions depending on the time of year and time of day. The wide, gentle surf of Coronado is a different world from the sea caves of La Jolla. Pacific Beach in July at noon is a different proposition from Torrey Pines on a Tuesday morning in March.

This guide covers the best beaches in San Diego in 2026 — with practical notes on parking, best visiting times, and what makes each one worth the trip.

The Best Beaches, Beach by Beach

La Jolla Cove — Wildlife, Clarity, and Sea Caves

La Jolla Cove is one of the most photographed spots in San Diego for good reason: the combination of cobalt-clear water, resident sea lions, and the dramatic sandstone cliffs backing the cove creates a scene that remains striking no matter how many times you've seen it. The cove itself is protected as an ecological preserve, which means the water is unusually clear and the marine life — leopard sharks (harmless), bright garibaldi fish, seals, and sea lions — is abundant and visible from the surface.

Snorkeling here is excellent on calm days. The sea caves to the south of the cove, accessible by kayak or paddleboard rental from nearby outfitters, are a worthwhile expedition for anyone comfortable on the water. The Children's Pool nearby — originally built as a protected swimming area but now a sea lion haul-out — is worth a stop even if you're not getting in the water.

Practical notes:
  • Parking: Street parking on Coast Boulevard fills quickly on weekends — arrive before 9am or after 4pm. The pay lot a few blocks away on Girard is more reliable.
  • Best time to visit: Weekday mornings for calm water and thinner crowds. July–September for warmest ocean temperatures.
  • Note: The cove is a protected ecological reserve. No disturbing marine life.

Coronado Beach — Wide, Calm, and Consistently Beautiful

Coronado Beach is the most polished of San Diego's coastal options. The sand here is unusually fine and has a subtle golden shimmer — the result of trace amounts of mica in the sand that catches afternoon light differently than most Pacific beaches. The Hotel del Coronado, a Victorian resort dating to 1888, stands at the northern end of the main beach and gives the whole stretch an air of occasion.

The surf at Coronado is gentler than at the open Pacific beaches — waves are consistent but manageable, making it one of the better options for swimmers who aren't experienced in heavy surf. The beach is wide enough that even busy summer days don't feel cramped. The village of Coronado, a short walk from the sand, has good coffee shops, ice cream, and restaurants for rounding out a beach day.

Practical notes:
  • Getting there: Cross via the Coronado Bridge (tolls apply inbound from SD) or take the ferry from the Broadway Pier — the 15-minute crossing is itself a pleasant part of the day.
  • Parking: Free street parking along Ocean Boulevard and in the adjacent residential blocks. Fills by mid-morning on summer weekends.
  • Best time to visit: Late morning through afternoon for warmth. Spring and fall for fewer crowds with comfortable temperatures.

Pacific Beach — Energy, Boardwalk, and the Classic San Diego Scene

Pacific Beach — PB to locals — is San Diego's most socially active stretch of coastline. The boardwalk runs the full length of the beach and functions as a continuous public gathering place: cyclists, skaters, joggers, volleyball games, and a concentration of restaurants and bars that make it easy to turn a beach afternoon into a full day out. The pier at Crystal Pier is one of the few in San Diego where you can actually rent a cottage perched over the water.

The surf at PB is more active than Coronado, which makes it a better option for bodysurfing and beginner board surfing — several surf schools and rental shops operate along Garnet Avenue and the boardwalk. The crowd skews younger and the energy is accordingly louder, especially on summer weekends. Weekday mornings, before the day gets rolling, are a different and quieter experience.

Practical notes:
  • Parking: Paid lots at Belmont Park and along the boardwalk. Street parking on the residential blocks east of Mission Boulevard — can require a 10–15 minute walk on busy days.
  • Best time to visit: Early morning for a quieter run or swim. Late afternoon for the social scene and sunset views. Avoid midday Saturday in July.

Mission Beach — Compact, Walkable, and Genuinely Fun

Mission Beach sits between Pacific Beach to the north and Mission Bay to the east, occupying a narrow strip of land that gives it an unusual density of activity for a beach neighborhood. The boardwalk here is continuous with the PB boardwalk and equally lively. Belmont Park, an old-school beachfront amusement park with a historic wooden roller coaster, anchors the southern end of the beach strip and is a reliable draw for families and anyone who enjoys that particular flavor of coastal nostalgia.

The ocean side at Mission Beach has good surf — intermediate and experienced surfers do well here — while the bay side offers calm, flat water for kayaking, paddleboarding, and swimming without wave management. The combination of ocean and bay access within walking distance of each other makes Mission Beach unusually versatile for a single-day outing.

Practical notes:
  • Parking: Limited and competitive on summer weekends. The lots around Belmont Park fill early. Arriving by rideshare or bike from adjacent neighborhoods is a practical alternative.
  • Best time to visit: Any day outside peak summer for easier access. Summer evenings for the boardwalk scene.

Del Mar Beach — Quieter, Upscale, and Dog-Friendly

Del Mar sits about 20 miles north of downtown San Diego, and its beach has a noticeably different character from the city beaches to the south. The pace is quieter, the cliff-top homes above the sand are architecturally impressive, and the dog beach at the north end is one of the more popular off-leash ocean spots in the county. The Del Mar Fairgrounds, home to the famed Del Mar Thoroughbred Club racing season (July–September), sits just east of the beach and draws additional visitors to the area through summer.

The town of Del Mar itself is a ten-minute walk from the beach and has some of the better casual restaurant and coffee options in the county's North Coastal communities. The combination of beach access, a walkable village, and calmer weekend crowds than the city beaches makes Del Mar worth the drive.

Practical notes:
  • Parking: Metered lots at Seagrove Park and along 15th Street. Less competition than city beaches, but still fills by late morning on weekends.
  • Dog beach: North of 29th Street — off-leash rules apply. Dogs must be on leash south of 29th. Rules change seasonally (check City of Del Mar website).
  • Best time to visit: Weekday mornings, or any fall/winter day when the weather cooperates.

Torrey Pines State Beach — The Wildest Stretch of San Diego Coast

Torrey Pines State Beach, below the reserve of the same name, is the most naturally dramatic of San Diego's accessible beaches. Towering sandstone bluffs rise above the sand, the Torrey Pines Golf Course runs along the clifftops, and the beach itself can stretch for wide, uncrowded miles at low tide. The dramatic cliffs at Flat Rock are worth the walk down from the parking area at the south end of the beach.

The beach sits at the base of Torrey Pines State Natural Reserve — one of the last natural stands of the rare Torrey pine tree — and the trails that descend from the reserve to the beach create a genuinely rare combination of coastal hiking and beach access in the same outing. Hang gliders launch from the cliffs at the adjacent Torrey Pines Gliderport regularly, adding a visual element that's particular to this stretch of coast.

Practical notes:
  • Parking: Pay lots at the state beach entrance and at Torrey Pines State Reserve (fee required). Arrive early on weekends — both lots fill. Street parking along North Torrey Pines Road is an option for those willing to walk.
  • Best time to visit: Low tide for the widest beach and easiest access to Flat Rock. Winter and spring mornings for clear air and almost no crowds.
  • Note: Part of a state reserve — no dogs on the beach. Fires not permitted.

A Few Notes on Timing San Diego Beaches

San Diego's famous "June Gloom" — a marine layer that typically blankets the coast from late May through June — means overcast mornings are common in late spring. The afternoons often clear, and the period from July through October brings the most reliably sunny beach days. That said, the shoulder seasons (late September through November, February through April) often produce spectacular clear days with far thinner crowds than summer peak.

Water temperatures in San Diego peak in late summer at around 70°F (21°C) and drop to the low-60s in winter — cold enough to make a wetsuit worthwhile for anyone spending extended time in the water outside of July–October.
